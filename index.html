Core Negotiation Mental Models

These foundational frameworks underpin every successful negotiation. They complement the decision-making principles at KeepRule Principles.

1. BATNA -- Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement

The Most Important Concept in Negotiation

Your BATNA is what you will do if the current negotiation fails. It is your walkaway power -- the foundation of all negotiating strength. The party with the better BATNA holds the stronger position.

How to Develop Your BATNA

  • List all alternatives available if this deal falls through
  • Evaluate each alternative realistically (not optimistically)
  • Select the best alternative as your BATNA
  • Improve your BATNA before the negotiation begins
  • Never reveal your BATNA unless it strengthens your position

Real-World Example

A job candidate with two competing offers has a strong BATNA. They can negotiate salary confidently because walking away does not mean unemployment -- it means taking the other offer. This changes the entire dynamic. Learn more about leveraging alternatives at KeepRule Scenarios.

2. ZOPA -- Zone of Possible Agreement

Finding the Space Where Deals Happen

The ZOPA is the range between each party's reservation price (the worst deal they would accept). If the zones overlap, a deal is possible. If they do not overlap, no agreement can satisfy both parties.

Buyer Range: $300K - $450K
Seller Range: $380K - $500K
ZOPA: $380K - $450K

The key insight: understanding the ZOPA helps you set realistic expectations and identify creative solutions when the initial zones do not overlap.

3. Anchoring Effect in Negotiation

The Power of the First Number

The first number put on the table disproportionately influences the final outcome. Research shows that final agreements tend to cluster around the anchor, even when the anchor is arbitrary.

When to Anchor First

Make the first offer when you have good information about the ZOPA. Your anchor pulls the negotiation toward your target. However, if you lack information, letting the other side anchor first can reveal valuable data about their expectations.

Application

In salary negotiations, research by behavioral economists featured on KeepRule shows that candidates who name a specific (high but reasonable) number first typically end up with 5-10% higher salaries than those who let the employer anchor first.

4. Win-Win (Integrative) Negotiation

Expanding the Pie Before Dividing It

Most negotiations are not purely zero-sum. By understanding each party's underlying interests (not just their positions), you can often find creative solutions that make both sides better off.

The Interest-Based Approach

  • Separate people from the problem -- Address emotions and relationships separately from substantive issues
  • Focus on interests, not positions -- Ask WHY someone wants something, not just WHAT they want
  • Generate options for mutual gain -- Brainstorm before committing; creativity creates value
  • Use objective criteria -- Base agreements on fair standards, not power or pressure

5. Reservation Price and Aspiration Price

Know Your Numbers Before You Negotiate

Your reservation price is the worst deal you would accept (your walkaway point). Your aspiration price is the best deal you realistically hope to achieve. The gap between them defines your negotiation range.

Reservation Price

The absolute minimum (or maximum) you will accept. Walking away is better than accepting a deal worse than this. Set it before negotiations begin and stick to it. Use frameworks from KeepRule Principles to set rational reservation prices.

Aspiration Price

Your optimistic but realistic target. Research shows that negotiators with higher aspirations achieve better outcomes -- as long as the aspiration is grounded in reality and not so extreme that it offends the other party.

6. The Reciprocity Principle

Give to Get

People feel obligated to return favors and concessions. Making a concession (especially an early, visible one) creates social pressure for the other side to reciprocate. This is one of the most powerful forces in negotiation.

Strategic concessions should be planned in advance. Start with concessions that cost you little but are valuable to the other side. This maximizes the reciprocity effect while minimizing your actual cost.

7. Information Asymmetry

Knowledge Is Negotiating Power

The party with more information almost always achieves better outcomes. Preparation is the single most important factor in negotiation success -- yet most people spend more time negotiating than preparing.

Before any negotiation, research: the other party's constraints, their alternatives, their decision-making process, industry benchmarks, and recent comparable deals. This information shapes your strategy, anchors, and BATNA assessment.

Preparation Checklist

Use structured decision frameworks like those at KeepRule Scenarios to prepare systematically. Know your BATNA, their likely BATNA, the ZOPA, your aspiration and reservation prices, and your planned concession sequence.

8. Commitment and Consistency

Small Yeses Lead to Big Agreements

People prefer to act consistently with their prior commitments and statements. Getting small agreements early in a negotiation builds momentum toward the final deal. Each "yes" makes the next "yes" more likely.

Start by establishing shared principles, common goals, or agreed-upon facts. Build on these foundations progressively. This is why skilled negotiators spend significant time on relationship-building and common ground before discussing contentious issues.
